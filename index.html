Climate Change:

Taking responsibility and investing in action


Climate change is an existential threat to life on Earth. Catalyzed by successive waves of industrialization and accelerated by unchecked development and consumer culture, the effects of climate change are manifesting in an outsized burden on those least responsible and least prepared.


How did we get here and how can we invest equitably in solutions to this threat?

Introduction

The world is in the midst of a climate crisis. The effects of climate change are already being felt around the world, and the consequences are becoming more severe in the form of extreme weather events, rising sea levels, and rising temperatures. The climate crisis is not just a problem for the environment, it is a problem for humanity.

This story explores the inequitable international distribution of responsibility for climate change and the unequal burden it places on the world's most vulnerable populations. It also examines the distribution of climate finance and the persisting gaps in addressing the climate crisis.

01

Driving Forces

Greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions are the primary driving force of climate change. These gasses stay trapped in the atmosphere and disrupt the natural balance of heat and energy on Earth, leading to increasing extreme weather and shifting climate patterns that endanger lives and livelihoods. Global GHG emissions have drastically increased in the last two centuries, with certain groups of countries contributing far more than others.

GHG emissions in modern times reflect a dramatic and unnatural increase compared to normal rates over the course of Earth's history, with sharp acceleration in the last century.


Here emission levels are not solely determined by the actual emissions of CO2, methane, and other GHGs, but also by changes in land use that affect the balance of GHGs in the atmosphere, such as deforestation, wetland drainage, agricultural expansion, and unsustainable urban development.

The responsibility for elevated GHGs in the atmosphere is not evenly shared around the world. High and upper-middle income countries have contributed the majority of emissions. From 1850 to 2024, the two higher income groups cumulatively contributed 7x more emissions than the two lower income groups.


In 2024 alone, high and upper-middle income countries contributed 80% of global GHG emissions, while lower-middle and low income countries contributed only 16% and 4% of global GHG emissions, respectively.

The United States, China, and Russia are the highest historical emitters and fall within the high and upper-middle income groups–in line with overall trends. There are some notable outliers in the lower-middle and lower income groups: India and the Democratic Republic of Congo. These countries were colonies of European powers until the mid-20th century, so in truth, their land use and emission generating decisions were not made autonomously and their cumulative emissions for much of the study period could be attributed to their former colonizers.


Generally, despite contributing minimally to the climate crisis and benefiting the least from the capital gains associated with industrialization, poorer countries have already begun to shoulder the brunt of the effects of anthropogenic climate change.

02

Unequal Risk

This section explores global vulnerability to and preparedness for the impending effects of climate change. ND-GAIN's Country Index Score is one of the best sources of data on these metrics at a country level around the world. Let's break down the two components that inform the Country Index: Vulnerability and Readiness.

Vulnerability

Each country's vulnerability score is aggregated from a handful of industry sectors considered essential to life and how they score in terms of exposure to climate-related hazards, sensitivity to the impacts of those hazards, and adaptive capacity to cope or adapt to impacts.


Many lower income countries are located near the Equator or in small island states, which translates to extremely hot climates or close proximity to the ocean. This means lower income countries are at a disproportiantely greater risk to effects of climate change such as drought, extreme heat, and sea level rise. Many have already experienced devastating climate related catastrophes.

Readiness

This indicator begins to incorporate a country's ability to effectively utilize investments for adaptation capacity building. Readiness scores are determined by each country's ability to effectively invest in climate adaptation, or mobilize capital from the private sector toward resilience projects, the stability of the government and institutions which determine the risk of investment in said country, and the social preparedness to effectively and equitably use any invested capital for resilience.


Again, lower income countries tend to be less ready to utilize or receive investments with the purpose of facilitating adaptation projects, with a more noticeable lack in South America, Africa, and South Asia.

Country Index

The combination of these factors, Vulnerability and Readiness, informs the overall Country Index Scores, which represents each country's readiness to use investments to reduce its respective vulnerability to the dangers posed by the effects of climate change.


Here we see in sharp contrast the results of historical inequality, producing a reality in which the lowest income countries are vastly underprepared for the challenges they had little hand in creating, but are most endangered by.

03

Investing in Action

Though the subject of climate change often brings feelings of hopelessness and despair, it is essential to remember that there are scientifically backed interventions that can make a difference. These interventions are broadly categorized as:


Mitigation

Also known as decarbonization, mitigation is the process of reducing present and future GHG emissions, as well as removing or capturing existing atmospheric GHGs.

Adaptation

Adaptation is the process of improving system capacity to withstand and thrive under the impacts of climate change.

Using data from the Climate Policy Initiative, we can see how capital is flowing on a global scale towards supporting mitigation and adaptation efforts.

Mitigation

As of the most recent data from 2023, the vast majority of climate finance is flowing toward mitigation initiatives, in the order of USD $1.8 trillion.


China stands out for its mobilization of approximately $680 billion toward mitigation initiatives, though this capital is mainly going towards projects within China. Despite their largely internal investments in mitigation efforts and historical position as second-highest GHG emitting country, Chinese innovation in clean energy and transition technology is globally beneficial for their advancements in the field.


Mitigation interventions take the form of renewable energy projects, energy efficiency improvements, and carbon capture and storage technologies.

Adaptation

The vastly reduced magnitude of investment in adaptation initiatives compared to mitigation is a source of concern for many experts in the field. While mitigation is essential for limiting global temperature rise to a 1.5°C increase by the end of the century, adaptation is integral to the continued functioning of human societies and institutional structures as the impacts of climate change continue to intensify.


Investments in adaptation go towards interventions such as early warning systems, nature-based solutions to mitigate heat and flooding, controlled burns to prevent wildfires, and drought resistant agriculture practices.

Dual Benefit

Dual benefit projects are those that support both mitigation and adaptation efforts, offering a more comprehensive and integrated approach to addressing climate change and its effects.


Interventions that provide dual benefits include wetland restoration, which sequesters carbon, protects against flooding, and supports natural ecosystems, solar panels over cropland, which generates renewable energy and protects crops from extreme weather conditions like high heat and hail, and reflective roofs, which improve home cooling efficiency and reduce urban heat islands.

Conclusion

Confronting the effects of climate change and preventing further damage is the battle of our generation and future generations to come, with every living being on Earth's existence in the balance. Thankfully, we are not helpless in this fight. Every year, more capital is invested in climate finance initiatives supporting both mitigation and adaptation efforts, with CPI reporting that climate investments could achieve the minimum estimate of need of USD $6 trillion annually by 2028 if growth remains constant. And growth will only remain constant if we all continue to do our part in prioritizing climate action.

More importantly, we must ensure that our climate actions are equitable and just, leaving no one behind in the fight against climate change. Though political prioritization of climate action may vary, we must remain committed to advocating for effective policy and committing time and resources to supporting climate action, especially for those in our communities and beyond who are most at risk.

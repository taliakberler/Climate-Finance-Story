Introduction

The world is in the midst of a climate crisis. The effects of climate change are already being felt around the world, and the consequences are becoming more severe in the form of extreme weather events, rising sea levels, and rising temperatures. The climate crisis is not just a problem for the environment, it is a problem for humanity.

This story explores the sources of climate change and the unequal burden it places on the world's most vulnerable populations. Further, it is about the response to the climate crisis and the approaches to mitigation and adaptation that are being put in place, though there is still a great deal of work to be done.

01

Driving Forces

Greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions are the primary driving force of climate change, as these gasses stay trapped in the atmosphere and disrupt the natural balance of heat and energy on Earth, leading to increasing extreme weather and shifting climate patterns that endanger lives and livelihoods. Global GHG emissions have drastically increased in the last two centuries, with certain groups of countries contributing far more than others.

GHG emissions in modern times reflect a dramatic and unnatural increase compared to normal rates over the course of Earth's history, with sharp acceleration in the last century. GHG emissions are determined here not just by the actual emissions of CO2, methane, and other GHGs, but also by changes in land use that affect the balance of GHGs in the atmosphere, like deforestation.

The responsibility for increased GHG emissions is not evenly shared around the world. High and upper-middle income countries have historically contributed the majority of emissions, with the United States and China holding the ranks of first and second highest historical emittors, respectively.


As of 2024, higher income countries were still responsible for 80% of global GHG emissions, while lower-middle and low income countries contributed the least, with 16% and 4% of world GHG emissions, respectively.

Many of the lower income and low emitting countries are located near the Equator or in small island states in Central and South America, Africa, and Asia. More often than not, these countries are located in extremely hot biomes or in close proximity to the ocean, which puts them at greater risk to the effects of climate change.


Despite contributing minimally to the climate crisis and benefiting the least from the associated industrialization, poorer countries have already begun to shoulder the burden of anthropogenic climate change. These lower income countries are being hit first and hardest by drought, extreme heat, sea level rise, and other effects of climate change.

02

Unequal Risk

Here we explore global vulnerability to and preparedness for the impending effects of climate change. ND-GAIN's Country Index Score is one of the best sources of data on these metrics at a country level around the world. Let's break down the two components that inform the Country Index: Vulnerability and Readiness.

Vulnerability

Each country's vulnerability score is aggregated from a handful of industry sectors considered essential to life and how they score in terms of exposure to climate-related hazards, sensitivity to the impacts of those hazards, and adaptive capacity to cope or adapt to impacts. Low income countries, which includes many countries in Africa and small island developing states (SIDS) are the most vulnerable to the effects of climate change at present.

Readiness

This indicator begins to incorporate a country's ability to effectively utilize investments for adaptation capacity building. Readiness scores are determined by each country's ability to effectively invest in climate adaptation, or mobilize capital from the private sector toward resilience projects, the stability of the government and institutions which determines the risk of investment in said country, and the social preparedness to effectively and equitably use any invested capital for resilience. Again, lower income countries tend to be less ready to utilize or receive investments with the purpose of facilitating adaptation projects, with a more noticeable lack in South America, Africa, and South Asia.

Country Index

The combination of these factors, Vulnerability and Readiness, informs the overall Country Index Scores, which represents each country's readiness to use investments to reduce its respective vulnerability to the dangers posed by the effects of climate change. Here we see in sharp contrast the results of historical inequality, producing a reality in which the lowest income countries are vastly underprepared for the challenges they had little hand in creating, but are most endangered by.


This bleak situation begs the question: what is being done to help?

03

Mitigation & Resilience

Though the subject of climate change often brings feelings of despair and hopelessness, it is essential to remember that we can always:

  1. Reduce emissions to reduce the future impacts of climate change
  2. Improve societal and institutional capacity to withstand and recuperate from climate change induced disasters

These two forms of action in the face of climate change are referred to, respectively, as Mitigation and Resilience. Using data from the Climate Policy Initiative, we can see where finance is flowing towards these two initiatives geographically and from an economic development perspective.

Introduction

The world is in the midst of a climate crisis. The effects of climate change are already being felt around the world, and the consequences are becoming more severe in the form of extreme weather events, rising sea levels, and rising temperatures. The climate crisis is not just a problem for the environment, it is a problem for humanity.

This story is about the sources of climate change and the unequal burden it places on the world's most vulnerable populations. Further, it is about the response to the climate crisis and the approaches to mitigation and adaptation that are being put in place, though there is still a great deal of work to be done.

01

Driving Forces

Greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions are the primary driving force of climate change, as these gasses stay trapped in the atmosphere and disrupt the natural balance of heat and energy on Earth. This sections explores how global GHG emissions have drastically increased in the last two centuries, with certain groups of countries contributing far more than others.

The global trend of greenhouse gas emissions reflects a dramatic increase since the advent of the industrial revolution, with sharp acceleration in the last century. GHG emissions are measured in metric tonnes of carbon dioxide-equivalents over a 100-year timescale and account for land use changes.

These emissions are not evenly distributed across the world. High and upper-middle income countries have contributed the majority of emissions reaching 80% of world emissions in 2024, while lower-middle and low income countries contributed the least, with 16% and 4% of world emissions, respectively.

Higher income countries tend to be higher emitters and are located at higher latitudes than lower income countries, which are characteristically lower emitters and located closer to the Equator.


Thus, lower income countries are being hit first and hardest by drought, extreme heat, sea level rise, and other effects of climate change. Despite contributing minimally to the climate crisis and benefiting the least from the associated industrialization, poorer countries have already begun to shoulder the burden of anthropogenic climate change.
